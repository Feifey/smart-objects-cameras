## Why this person?

Two or three sentences. Who did you pick and why? You're looking for someone who **doesn't know much about the class** — not another student, not someone you've already shown the project to a dozen times. A roommate, a sibling, a friend in another program, a parent on FaceTime, a classmate from an unrelated class — anyone who can react with fresh eyes.

The point of this assignment is to find the things you take for granted. People inside the class can't see those because they're inside it too.

## What you showed them

Two or three sentences. How did you introduce the project to them? Did you give them a one-line setup, or just hit play? Did they watch a demo, see screenshots, watch a video, see it running live? **Important:** try to show, don't explain — the less you front-load, the more honest their reaction.

> Example: *I showed Sam a 30-second clip of the timer reacting when I flipped the arUco tag, with no setup other than "watch this." After the clip ended I asked what he thought was happening before I told him anything.*

## The conversation

The questions below are a starting set — use them, modify them, skip what doesn't fit, and **add at least one of your own**. Aim for **6–10 exchanges**.

Capture answers in their **actual voice**. Quote them directly when they say something interesting, confused, or unexpected. Don't paraphrase into engineering-speak. *"Wait, the camera's the brain?"* is more useful than *"the interviewee inquired about the role of the camera in the system architecture."*

## What I learned that I couldn't have learned from a classmate

One short paragraph. **This is the most valuable part of the assignment.**

What did your interviewee notice, misread, or push back on that nobody inside the class would have flagged? What assumption did you realize you'd been making? Did they reframe the project in a way you hadn't considered? Did they hate something you thought was the best part?

Be honest, especially if the feedback stung — that's usually the most useful kind.

> Example: *Sam thought the timer was "yelling at students to focus" because the Discord notification felt like a teacher's voice. I'd been thinking of the bot's messages as neutral status updates, but he heard them as authoritative — and once he said it I couldn't unhear it. That's a tone problem I would never have caught from another classmate, because we all read those messages as ambient by now.*
